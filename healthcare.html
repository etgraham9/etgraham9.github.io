THE REALITY

U.S. HEALTHCARE CRISIS
01
Spending

The U.S. Spends More Than Any Nation on Earth

The United States spends over $4.5 trillion annually on healthcare — roughly $13,500 per person — yet ranks poorly among wealthy nations on outcomes like life expectancy, infant mortality, and preventable deaths. Americans pay twice as much as Canadians and Germans for comparable care.

$13,500+ per capita annually (2023, CMS)
02
Bankruptcy

Medical Debt Is the #1 Cause of Bankruptcy

More than half of U.S. personal bankruptcies are tied to medical debt, even among people who have insurance. About 100 million Americans carry medical debt — with one in four adults reporting they have skipped or delayed care because of cost fears. Medical bills push over 500,000 families into bankruptcy each year.

~$220 billion in medical debt held by Americans (CFPB, 2022)
03
Pricing Opacity

Prices Are Hidden by Design

Unlike any other developed country, the U.S. lacks a standard pricing system for medical care. The same MRI can cost $400 at one facility and $4,000 at another across the street. Hospitals are now legally required to post prices — but compliance is inconsistent, and the data is often incomprehensible to everyday patients.

Price variation for same procedure: up to 10x within the same city
04
Insurance

Having Insurance Doesn't Mean You're Protected

25 million Americans remain uninsured, but even insured patients face crushing costs from high deductibles, surprise billing, narrow networks, and denied claims. The average deductible for employer-sponsored insurance now exceeds $1,600. Nearly 40% of insured adults said they struggled to afford their deductible last year.

Avg. employer plan deductible: $1,644 (KFF, 2023)
05
Systemic

It's a System Problem, Not an Accident

High healthcare costs in the U.S. are driven by a complex web of factors: lack of price negotiation, administrative overhead (up to 34% of hospital costs), pharmaceutical pricing, hospital consolidation, and a fee-for-service model that rewards more procedures rather than better outcomes. Many healthcare economists argue that meaningful reform requires structural change — not just expanded coverage.

Administrative costs: 34.2% of total hospital expenditures (NEJM)
A Note

The prices in this game reflect average cash-pay costs for uninsured patients, drawn from sources including FAIR Health, Healthcare Bluebook, and CMS data. Actual costs vary significantly by location, insurance status, and facility type. The purpose of this game is not to scare — it's to inform. A well-functioning healthcare system should not require patients to be financial experts to survive it.

Prices sourced from FAIR Health, CMS, Healthcare Bluebook, and peer-reviewed literature. For educational purposes.

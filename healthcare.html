THE REALITY

U.S. HEALTHCARE INJUSTICE
01
The Issue

America Pays the Most and Gets the Least Back

The United States spends more on healthcare than any other country, on average more than $13,500 per person each year. Despite this, Americans die younger, lose more babies at birth, and face more preventable deaths than people in other comparable countries that spend much less. In the US around 25 million people are without insurance and one in four adults skip doctors visits because they cannot afford to go. Sickness in American can lead to complete financial disaster.

$4.5 trillion in annual U.S. healthcare spending — yet 1 in 4 adults skip care due to cost (KFF, 2023)
02
Identity & Structural Cause

Your Zip Code and Skin Color Shouldn't Determine Your Care

In America, who you are can often predict your health coverage and outcomes, your race, income, and job largely determine the quality and frequency. Black and Latino Americans are uninsured at nearly twice the rate that white Americans are. Low income workers, who are disproportionately people of color due to other structural issues not covered on this website, are the least likely to receive coverage through their jobs. This stems from deliberate choices created from hundreds of years of racist societal structures by those in charge. Early labor laws excluded domestic and farm workers from having protections and housing segregation concentrated poverty and poor health resources inside of communities of color. The end state of this is a world in which the color of your skin directly predicts your survival.

Uninsured rate: 5.4% white adults vs. 17.7% Latino adults (KFF, 2023)
03
Injustice & Analysis

You Shouldn't Go Bankrupt for Getting Sick

The US is the only wealthy country in which sickness can erase your savings. Over 500,000 families file for bankruptcy each year as a result of medical bills, some of them despite having insurance. While hospitals are technically required to post their prices, many don’t comply, and when they are released the files are often unintuitive and difficult to interpret for a layman. Real change would involve extricating medical care from financial status whether it’s through enforced transparency, price caps, expanding Medicaid, or reining in private equity firms who acquire hospitals and drive up the costs.

~$220 billion in outstanding medical debt held by Americans (CFPB, 2022)
04
Current Strategies

Progress Has Been Made but It's Not Enough

Things have gotten better, albeit slowly. The ACA, Affordable Care Act, brought coverage to millions of uninsured Americans and worked to stop insurers from rejecting people with preexisting conditions. The Inflation Reduction Act helped lower medical costs by capping insulin at 35$ a month, though this only impacted Medicare patients. The No Surprises Act worked to address unexpected out of network bills, in network coverage being an often brought up issue within healthcare. Despite all this good, the ACA left 25 million people without coverage, and the insulin cap doesn’t help those uninsured. Additionally, surprise billing rules are still being fought in court by the healthcare industry leaving the act's future in question. At its core, the real problem, an opaque pricing system shaped by powerful lobbies, compounded by the structural factors that create racism and economic hardship haven’t truly been touched.

Uninsured rate: 18% (2013) → 8% (2023) post-ACA - but stalled there (CDC)
05
An Alternative Strategy

A Practical Plan: Enforce Transparency & Put Navigators in the Room

A 2023 audit found that more than 30% of major hospitals were ignoring price transparency laws without consequence. My approach seeks to reduce that number and cut out some potential loopholes. First, state attorney generals need real power, like more staff and heavy fines, to hold uncompliant hospitals accountable. Secondly, there should be established healthcare navigators. People who are trained and paid to help advocate for patients within hospitals, clinics, and emergency rooms to decode bills and find assistance if needed before debt spirals out of hand. Third, a public database of hospital pricing and insurance comparisons would allow patients to make educated decisions when it comes to spending their money on healthcare. Organizations like Community Catalyst and state health departments could launch some of these programs now and success could be tracked through the amount of healthcare disputes and people filing for bankruptcy after medical treatment.

Price variation for same procedure: up to 10x within the same city
06
Why It Matters

You Can't Do Anything Else If You're Sick and Broke

Medical debt can be crushing and not addressing your medical needs can be even more so. Both impact your ability to keep a job, pay for school, staying housed, raising kids, and so much more. Healthcare is a human right and should be treated as such. The system has clearly failed and the harm is not wrought on an individual level and can impact multiple generations as parents and children can end up paying for each other's medical expenses depending on age. It’s something worth fighting for because we know it can be solved. Many comparably wealthy and some less so, have figured out how to run a medical system in which sickness is not synonymous with bankruptcy. The barriers surrounding this issue are quite literally the difference between life and death, and so we should treat them as such and seek to fix them as quick as possible.

U.S. life expectancy: 76.4 years, behind Canada (82), UK (81), and Germany (81) despite higher spending
A Note

The prices in this game reflect average cash pay costs for uninsured patients, pulled from FAIR Health, Healthcare Bluebook, and CMS data. Real costs vary a lot depending on where you live, what insurance you have, and what facility you use.
